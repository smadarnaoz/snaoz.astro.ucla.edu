Spiral Arms in the MilkyWay Galaxy

The word galaxy was derived from our own galaxy, the MilkyWay, using the Greek word "gala" which means milk. The band of light visible in the night sky, which is called the Milky Way, is actually the plane of the disk of the galaxy. In our universe there are countless stars and galaxies. Stars are almost always assembled in collections called galaxies, together with gas, dust, and dark matter. Galaxies are held together by gravitational attraction, and galactic components orbit a common center, most likely containing a black hole.

Edwin Hubble defined an extensive description of galaxy types but we can identify three main types: ellipticals, spirals, and irregulars. These types relate to the shape of the galaxy. The spiral type, which interests us, has spiral arms with the shape of approximate logarithmic spirals (similar to the shape of a spiral staircase).

Spiral structure has been an enigma to astrophysicists for many years. The most common theory today is that spiral structure can be regarded as a density wave, which revolves in the galactic disk in much the same way that waves propagate in the ocean. Lin & Shu (1964, 1966) were the first to apply the techniques of wave mechanics in order to deduce the properties of density waves. They developed a formalism called the density wave theory.

The stars in a galaxy have a differential velocity, which means that a star nearer to the center has a shorter period of rotation. Like the stars, the spiral arms also rotate around the center, but unlike the stars, they do so with constant angular velocity. This means that stars pass in and out of spiral arms. As gas in the interstellar medium passes into the density wave, it becomes denser, and this can lead to the formation of new stars. The very hottest (and brightest) stars have short lifetimes, so that they are born and die very close to the density wave. This is why the spiral arms are traced by the brightest and youngest stars.

Many interesting processes take place within spiral galaxies, where the spiral
arms, in particular, play an essential role. Apart from being the primary sites of star formation, spiral arms are important in determining the density, chemical composition (i.e., the abundance of carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, etc.), and temperature of the interstellar gas as well as the dynamics of stars and gas within the galaxy. It is for this reason that we wish to study the spiral arm dynamics.

The Milky Way contains about 300 billion stars and has a total mass of about
a trillion times the mass of the Sun, with a disk of about or 100,000 light years
in diameter and 3,000 light years in width. Furthermore there is some evidence to support the existence of a bar at the center of our galaxy (a bar is a band of high density emerging from the center and running across the middle of the galaxy). The spiral structure in our galaxy is divided into several observed arms, which are named after the location in which we view them in the sky, for example the Orion spiral arm is viewed close to the constellation Orion. Our solar system is located at the Orion "armlet", about two thirds of the radius of the disk from its center.

Today, no astronomer doubts that the Milky Way is a spiral galaxy. Still, our
edge-on view and dust obscuration through the galactic disk does not permit us to see the structure and pattern of the spiral arms directly. It is for this reason that it is difficult to unequivocally determine properties of the spiral arms in our galaxy. In my Master’s thesis work, my advisor, Nir Shaviv and I mapped and studied the kinematics and dynamics of the Milky Way’s spiral arms in our vicinity using the birth locations of open clusters; an open cluster is a small group of stars all of which are born at the same
time from a molecular cloud and are still near each other. Since these objects are located in the galactic plane and are born primarily in the arms, they are an excellent tool for analyzing the dynamics of the spiral structure. Using their birth locations and their distribution of ages, we found evidence for multiple pattern speeds. For the Carina spiral arm, we found that the data is best fitted with two pattern speeds, "faster" and "slower". In other words, the Carina arm actually consists of two different arms, which happen to co-exist in the same region. Furthermore we found that the "slower" Carina arm and the Perseus spiral arm are most probably moving in the same speed, which means that they are part of the same wave pattern. The solar system passes one arm of this set about every one hundred and forty million years. The speed of the Orion arm and the speed of the “faster” Carina arm are close to that of the solar system’s speed around the galactic center. Since our solar system is located in the Orion arm, it will leave this arm in about two hundred million years.


Shaviv (2002) suggested a possible connection between the passing of the solar
system in a spiral arm and Earth’s climate, especially a connection to the formation of clouds due to the arrival of particles generated near dying stars to the Earth’s upper atmosphere. Therefore our work may contribute to the understanding of Earth’s climate history, in addition to help us gain a better understanding of our own galaxy. Want to know more?
